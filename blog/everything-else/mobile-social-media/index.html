Mobile social media: the purge

November 3, 2025 - 11 min read

For about six weeks now, I have been living with no social media on my smart phone. I've never been the kind of social media person that posts frequently or has specific community use cases for social media, so why would I remove these apps from my phone? Maybe a better question to start is why would I have them in the first place?

Installed social media

I'm going to go through the social media apps that I had installed and what my original motivations were for installing them in the first place.

1. Facebook

I think this might be the "oldest" social media that I had. We remember the days of seeing what our friends and family were up to or thinking and I think that's part of the reason that I still had the Facebook app installed. Features like groups or marketplace I have found quite useful over the years. I'm a frugal person so buy/sell groups, buy nothing groups, and marketplace have been bastions of my monetary personality.

2. YouTube

YouTube videos have been part of my life for as long as I can remember YouTube existing. Much of my career and hobby knowledge was learned on YouTube and many of the DIY skills I have had their inception with YouTube. From cars to household tasks to homelabbing, YouTube has content out there for learning anything. Some of my favorite non-educational content creators are on YouTube as well. I've watched many hours of content on video games I never intended on playing simply because these creators are fun to watch. YouTube has been a staple for me.

3. Reddit

To the detriment of my mental health, Reddit was a good place to get news quickly for things happening around the world. I liked the idea of curated groups or topics of news and so Reddit drew me in. Of course Reddit isn't a news source itself, but by being an aggregate for news, you often see a full range of publishers being posted. Reddit is not primarily a news app though. There are many awesome communities out there for all other purposes.

4. Instagram

Photography is one of the few art forms that I've been consistently interested in. Instagram was originally a great place to find inspiration and learn new things from. Many of my favorite wildlife photographers I found on Instagram first.

Why remove them?

Something that is clear after listing those out is that each of those social medias have been used as a form of education, even Facebook with some of the groups that I've joined over the years, like the Sony Alpha group for photography. Social media over the years has become engineered to maximize our attention. Out of the four above, three have introduced short form video content and have placed it front and center within these platforms.

Short form video content is one of the more effective ways of maximizing attention on these apps. We've all been there: opened up Instagram to spy on one of our friends and 30 minutes of reels later, we forgot why we opened the app in the first place.

The other way that social media maximizes on attention is that "fringe" content is algorithmically promoted because those edgy forms of content produce the highest amount of engagement. Outrageous, crazy, borderline via the content policy: all good for engagement.

All of this to say: I have to fight for my attention any time I open any of these apps. Is this an issue of willpower or focus? I don't think so as I've heard the same from others. I think a better question though is why do I need to see these things on my phone? Just because I can doesn't mean I should. Much of the content I've mentioned is better consumed on a larger screen anyway. So why? Convenience? Fear of missing out? I actually don't know. Probably convenience, probably FOMO, but also easy access to dopamine.

I think I can try and come up with other justifications all day, but really I had these apps installed subconsciously as easy dopamine hits and I know I'm not alone. I had become desensitized to anything and everything having to do with social media, so I required more and more to satiate these impulses. This had adverse effects in real life too. These platforms are marvels of human engineering and I just felt out of control; a constant impulse to reach for my phone, continuous guilt, and overall anxiety. I could never feel present in any moment.

I can summarize the reason for removing all my social media apps as: I wanted to be in control again.

What I expected to happen

By removing these apps, what did I expect? To be honest, I expected a sense of relief. Relief from constantly having the urge to reach for my phone. Relief from getting sucked into content I had no intention of viewing. And relief from feeling guilty for spending so much time on my phone when I wanted to do other things.

I wish I had screenshots of my screen time from back then, but iOS only allows you to view screen time about four weeks back. I believe it averaged about 4-5 hours a day, so I also expected my screen time to go down.

Another thing that I expected was for me to become less informed about what's going on. I like being in the know, whether that's tech related, political, or any other areas of interest or importance. How much of the content I was consuming contributed to this though? I didn't really have an answer and going forward, measuring this is tough as well.

What actually happened

Removing these apps was incredibly easy. They were gone in less than a minute. What followed was not easy. Immediately I had this intense feeling of "now what?" I wouldn't call this feeling boredom, but more of a void. An attention void? For the next few days, I was constantly reaching for my phone, despite knowing there was nothing there for me to check.

My attention to things in real life did not get better. I did not get a sense of relief like I was expecting, the opposite actually: a sort of anxiety around being left out, not knowing what to do, and not knowing how to combat my compulsions that were not waning. This continued for a couple weeks pretty consistently. I've heard of dopamine detoxes and I think this is similar. I don't think there really is a replacement behavior for social media.

However, after the three week mark, I started to notice myself reaching a bit less for my phone. At about this time too, I started checking npr.org a couple times a day as a news source. This helped a tad and NPR isn't a firehose of news, so checking more than once or twice a day is kind of pointless and their "timeline" is chronological so once you've seen something, you don't get more content.

I found that my focus streaks started to get a bit longer, maybe 10-20% longer which was previously an order of single digit minutes at best, so a minor improvement, but noticeable. The anxiety that I mentioned above was still there, but had decreased a bit as well. Still no relief. My screen time had been reduced quite a bit which is something that I had anticipated.

I also found myself with nothing to do frequently. And what I mean by that is I wouldn't be on my phone, but I didn't have anything else to do. Of course I have projects and other things that I want to work on, but none of those felt like they could fill the void. I've unfortunately picked up a mobile game, which feels like a regression, but I didn't feel guilty about it. I would rather that be my crux than social media and I'm confident it's something I can easily address in the future. This still hadn't filled the void, but I noticed that feeling of needing something to do waning.

Where I'm at today

Was all of this for naught? I mentioned a bit of improvement around three weeks and guess what? That improvement continued. I can easily focus now for at least ten minutes without reaching for my phone. The urge is still there, but I feel in control finally. My anxiety around needing to check social media and that void feeling has dropped considerably, albeit not gone.

Another interesting side effect of this process is that my mentality towards online content, especially short form, has increasingly gotten more negative. Before I thought the issue was just me, but now I have clarity into the issue being much more systemic and I have a feeling of repulsion towards these contents. I think this is akin to going sugar free where afterwards, the sweet stuff actually tastes bad.

I expected to become less informed and that is the one thing that I did get right. I still follow some news as I mentioned above, but the articles are limited and broad in scoped compared to some of the more specialized subreddits that I had been visiting. I'm not sure if this is a good or bad thing yet, so time will tell.

I've mentioned FOMO a few times and to be honest, I've missed a tiny amount of what my friends and family are posting and that's it. Turns out, I wasn't really seeing much of those posts anyway in the sea of sponsored content.

I never got that sense of relief. Relief is usually coupled with a noticeable and immediate change, which I got, but relief wasn't the outcome. My screen time did decrease by about 50% which is proof of how much time I was spending on social media, about 2-3 hours a day! As I mentioned before, I believe I traded some time with some a mobile game, so that's something that will need to be addressed if I want to get my screen time to a minimum.

What I would do differently

If I were starting this over I would do a few things differently:

  1. Have something ready to fill the attention void, like a book. Preferably something you enjoy already.
  2. Set up your "intentional device" ahead of time so you don't miss out on things that are important to you. Note: be careful with this though!
  3. Do this with someone else to hold each other accountable.

What the future looks like

I want to introduce social media back into my life slowly, but not on my phone. Some of the utility that I had originally gained from these platforms I'm still looking for. One thing that I've been experimenting with in my life is what I like to call "intentional devices". That deserves its own post, but the idea is that we can leverage devices for what they are good at and originally intended for. I can use my laptop for most things that I wanted originally from social media. Even better, I can use some free tools to tailor my experience to exactly what I want. For example: I can use Social Focus on Firefox (available on other browsers) to hide the things I don't care about on Facebook. That's the power of using a browser as your entry point. I'm now able to use Marketplace without any of the distractions on the way in.

Closing thoughts

Ultimately, my journey is not over. Do I still reach for my phone? Yes. Do I still feel FOMO? Absolutely. Is my anxiety better or worse than when I started? Better, but it was worse for a few weeks. Do I regret removing social media from my phone? Not at all.

I can't recommend this process enough. Everyone should do it. Take control of your attention and your time. Your time is your life. Don't let these big companies rob you of your time and data.

- Balake, liberated from social media
