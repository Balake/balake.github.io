A not so complete guide to commuting by bicycle in Seattle

October 4, 2025 - 7 min read

Why?

So first of all, why would you commute by bicycle in Seattle? There are hills, cars, and it rains 9 months out of the year. Those are not the friendliest conditions for a bicycle. Driving usually feels safer, drier, and faster.

I'm sure you're aware of the benefits of cycling as well: physical activity, smaller carbon footprint, no parking fees, etc. So if the safety of cycling compared to driving, dryness, and speed of cycling as a commute option can be addressed, then there should be no reason why we can't all be commuting by bicycle!

Why listen to me?

You don't have to! But hear me out. I've been commuting by bicycle for about 4 and a half years now. I'm no veteran commuter, but I have had plenty of time to make changes to my process and a couple of opportunities to explore. I'll first go over my commuting history, which should give some context for how I've come up with the guidelines I've listed below.

Brief history

First time commuting

The first time I commuted by bicycle was back in 2021. My office hadn't issued any form of RTO (return to office) just yet, but my job required some interaction with hardware on premise. I lived only 4 miles away, but in terms of driving time, that was about 20-30 minutes on a good day. While not a bad driving commute, I had a hunch that cycling could be faster. It wasn't. I didn't know what routes to take besides what Google Maps told me. I felt like I was mostly dodging cars and hoping I didn't get hit for 30 minutes.

Over time, I adjusted my route to roads that had less traffic, like neighborhood greenways and safe streets. I gradually got faster and felt safer. I think one of my best times in was just 14 minutes. With higher safety comes confidence and speed.

Moving

I moved in 2024 and my commute went from 4 miles one way to 11 miles. Quite the jump! This brought quite a few challenges as my time to commute was over an hour. I applied some of the same techniques that I learned with my shorter commute and got to a place where my commutes were approaching the 40 minute mark.

New job

In April of 2025, I started my new job, yay! With this, my commute was updated to 7 miles one way. Luckily, about 6 of those miles were on the same route, but that didn't stop me from iterating and continuing to improve. You'll notice that the beginning of my route has changed despite most of the route being the same. I'll get into why down below.

How?

Okay, so there are three main facets that I think are important to commuting by bicycle for both safety and speed. The main facet is the route.

Routing

When it comes to routing, there's only so much you can do by looking at a map. When I'm coming up with a new route, the first place I look is the most obvious: Google Maps. Google's cycling route data is usually quite good and will often route you on bike friendly paths. This is a good place to get your initial route.

Once you have your initial route, the best thing to do is ride it! There are some questions at the back of your head that you should be looking to answer:

  1. How much traffic is there?
  2. Are there physical barriers between vehicles and cyclists?
  3. Are there other cyclists on parts of this route?
  4. Is this route physically possible for you?

Once you have a solid baseline answer to those questions, you can start to make alterations or explorations to optimize your route.

As an example, part of my initial route included a 3 mile stretch of Healthy Street. What I learned though is that traffic was bad on this street. Not all of it, but a good chunk of it. Turns out that the nearby school meant that parents were driving down this road to drop off their kids. I changed my route to go a couple blocks over, parallel to the Healthy Street and no more traffic!

Another example is my initial 11 mile route included what is known on Strava as the West Seattle Wall. Let me tell you: this hill is steep. I dreaded it every time as it was at mile 9 of 11. I could do it, but it made me not want to commute, that's for sure. Luckily, there were some alternatives at the cost of some more distance, but one ended up being about equal for time, so I made the switch.

Attention

The second facet to safe and speedy commuting by bicycle is paying attention. I know that sounds simple, but it really is important. One thing that people often don't realize is part of paying attention is listening. You can't see behind you unless you have mirrors and often times, you'll hear a car or pedestrian before you see them. This is why I highly discourage the use of headphones or even the bone conducting listening devices.

Double check for danger!

A pro tip here is to always double check for danger. There have been times where I look one way to cross a road, then look the other, and by the time I'm looking forward again, the first direction I looked is no longer safe because of a speeding vehicle. Really make sure that the direction you're traveling is safe.

One final tip about paying attention is traffic lights. Leading pedestrian interval (LPI) is a good opportunity to get out in front of vehicles that are looking to turn. Often times drivers will jump at a green light, but if you're already in the intersection, you're much more likely to be seen.

I can go on about interacting with vehicles, but that deserves its own post to be honest. Moral of the story is never trust any driver.

Gear

Last but not least, the third facet of safe and fast commuting is bicycle gear. First of all, the bike! You don't need a carbon road bike to commute. You want something that is reliable, but most importantly, comfortable. Comfort should be the utmost priority when selecting a bicycle. If your bike isn't comfortable, you're not going to want to ride it. This typically means a proper bike fitting at your local bike shop if you're unsure of what to look for. I haven't mentioned e-bikes, but they are a great way to make your ride more comfortable.

Clothes are also something to consider. Can you change at work easily? Are you the type of person that wants to shower? Most importantly, wear clothes that keep you dry, because dry means comfortable, and I mean dry in terms of rain and sweat. I have found personally that fishing sun shirts and a rain shell work for almost every scenario.

Lights

Get some lights! Even if it's not dark, cycling lights significantly increase your chances of being seen. I recommend taking a look at some of the lights that Fenix Lighting makes, as they are high quality and have easily replaceable, rechargeable batteries.

Lastly, I recommend at least a bike rack for your work belongings. I was the guy with a backpack for a couple years, but decided to get a rack one hot summer day and haven't looked back. Your spine and lumbar muscles will thank you.

Wrapping up

Commuting by bicycle in Seattle is a lot of fun. Health benefits aside, you'll be polluting less, and supporting others that commute simply by being out there. The city of Seattle collects data on the number of cyclists and the more there are, the more allocation of resources goes towards cycling infrastructure.

Happy commuting!

- Blake, bicycling commuter
